Fez

The Imperial City

Fez has a special atmosphere. This 13th-century city is an imperial city of long-lasting multicultural history, that reserves unexpected surprises for its visitors. Cross the walls of the famous medina of Fez, recognized by UNESCO as an outstanding universal value. Stroll through the streets of the Fez-El-Bali district. The Bab Boujloud is the easiest way to enter the medina.

In early May, the squares and alleys resound with world music, where the Festival of World Sacred Music is held in the former imperial capital, which occurs along with Jazz in Riads, one of the main events in the city's cultural calendar. Don't leave without having tasted the city's gastronomy, reputed to be one of the best in the world.

What to see in Fez :

Quaraouiyine Mosque

Al Quaraouiyine Mosque is known to house the oldest university in the world ,one of the most important centers of Islamic learning.

Built in 859, under the mandate of Fátima al Fihriya, and in constant expansion since its construction, the Al Quaraouiyine Mosque, also known as Al Qarawiyyin, is one of the largest architectural ensembles in Fez.

Location : Between the Mausoleum of Moulay Idriss and the Place Seffarine.
* Nearby places :

Medersa Attarine (49 m) Place Seffarine (88 m) Medersa Cherratine (94 m) Mausoleum of Moulay Idriss (136 m) Museum of Wood Arts and Crafts (231 m)

Sultan Abu Inan endowed the Quaraouiyine University with a library with a very rich content which, in 1613, exceeded the sum of 320,000 works and manuscripts . It was then that students and scholars began to settle in Fez, which has become the capital of science and knowledge in the Maghreb.

Today, the library still preserves more than 30,000 works , including a few rare copies and manuscripts unique in the world .

To see her from the door

Despite the remarkable extension of the Al Quaraouiyine Mosque, it is difficult to find it because, over the centuries, the houses of the district have covered the perimeter of the building, thus concealing its shape .

As with the rest of the mosques in Fez, only people of the Muslim faith can access the interior of the Quaraouiyine mosque . Tourists must therefore be content with the small space visible from the door.

Médersa Attarine

The Medersa Attarine was one of the main schools of Koranic religion in which students could reside during their studies. As a large part of the classes took place in the Al Quaraouiyine mosque , most of the city's medersas (or madrasas) were located in its surroundings.

This Attarine madrassah, so called because of the district in which it is located, remained in use until the beginning of the 20th century , when the 60 students who resided in the 30 rooms had to abandon them for good.

The interior of the medersa

The medersa extends around an interior courtyard in the middle of which stands an imposing marble fountain . At the end of the patio, a cedar wood dome with abundant ornaments covers the prayer room in which you can contemplate the mihrab indicating the direction of Mecca. The walls of the Madrasah are decorated with beautiful mosaics on which are inscribed phrases from the Koran .

On the upper part of the walls you can admire impressive floral patterns. The upper floors of the Medersa Attarine are occupied by the small rooms in which the young students spent their years of study.

Although in Fez, you can visit other madrasahs, Attarine stands out above all for the elegance and harmony of its architecture . It is one of the best preserved and best decorated medersas in Morocco.

Next to the Karaouiyine mosque.
Schedule

Daily: 9:30 a.m. to 6 p.m. The Medersa Attarine will remain closed during prayer times.

Price
General admission : 20dh (2.20 US $).
Bab Boujloud Gate

The Bab Boujloud gate is a spectacular gate, made up of three symmetrical arches decorated with magnificent shades of blue .

Built in 1913, the Bab Boujloud is the main access to the Medina Fez el-Bali , the district 's oldest and most charming of the city. The Bab Boujloud gate is also known as "the blue gate".

Situation of Bab Boujloud

Passing through the gate, you will access the medina from the northwest, where the road branches left to enter Talaa Kebira (the big descent) and right into Talaa Seghira (The small slope), two of the main commercial arteries. from the city.

The liveliest district of Fez

Bab Boujloud is a bustling city full of people at all times of the day . It is therefore a fairly safe neighborhood , even at night.

One of the best ways to contemplate the ceaseless activity that prevails in this part of the city is to go up on the terrace of one of the restaurants or cafes nearby . It is a real pleasure to contemplate from top to bottom the comings and goings of the donkeys loading products for sale in the medina, or the people who stop to chat while shopping.

Northwest of the medina Fez el-Bali.
Chouara tannery

The Chouwara tannery offers, along with the other tanneries, one of the most picturesque and smelly panoramas in the city .

The Chouara tannery is the largest of the four traditional tanneries still present in the heart of the Fez el-Bali medina . There you will find an infinity of pits filled with natural dyes in charge of the production and coloring of the leather of lamb, beef, goat and camel.

Leather making process

The first step in traditional tanneries is to introduce the skins into large vats filled with lime and pigeon droppings , where they rest for several days.

Then the remaining hairs are removed and the skins are colored by introducing them into large vats filled with natural dyes.

Once the skins are colored and dry, they will be passed on to the artisans who will transform them into bags, suitcases, shoes and coats for sale to the public.

Show of colors and smells

The tanneries are surrounded by shops with terraces from which you can observe the hard work of the artisans . When entering the tanneries, you will receive small sprigs of mint which barely serve to alleviate the very strong and often unpleasant odor .

Despite the unbearable stench, the Chouara tannery is one of the most typical and representative places of the city and it is essential to visit it to get to know Fez . It is very common for sellers to try to get tourists to buy a piece of leather to watch this show, but with a small tip they will be satisfied.

Chouara Street, Fez.
Royal Palace of Fez

The royal palace of Fez, built in the 14th century, is one of the largest and oldest in Morocco .

Due to its large size, this palace was built outside the old medina. A new medina, Fès el-Jdid , was therefore created to meet the needs of the palace . Nearby is the Jewish quarter (Mellah) which, after having experienced days of glory, is currently in poor condition.

We will stay at the gates

Like most mosques and Moroccan palaces, it is impossible to visit the royal palace, or even to contemplate its exteriors. Once again, visitors must be content with the view of the impressive gates that lead to the palace : the seven gates of different sizes represent the seven days of the week and the seven levels of the monarchy.

The enormous bronze doors are framed by thousands of small ceramic tiles , on which the blue colors of Fez and the green of Islam predominate.

The palace and its surroundings

Going to the gates of the royal palace in Fez is worthwhile, not only to contemplate its grandeur, but also to visit the neighborhoods that surround it and discover its contrasts. The nearby Jewish Quarter houses offer a completely different aspect of the Muslim style. The old grain market is another point of interest, which will take you back in time. It is located behind the Bab Sammarine gate.

Rue Bou Ksissat, Fès el-Jdid .
Jewish quarter of Fez (Mellah)

Located in the heart of the medina of Fez el-Jdid , next to the royal palace , the "Mellah" is one of the most traditional and oldest districts of Fez.

It was for centuries the place of residence of the Jewish community .

Interesting facts about the mellah :

The name " Mellah ", which later gave its name to the rest of the Moroccan Jewish quarters, comes from the word "salt" in Arabic , because salt was of great value in ancient times and the Jews used it as currency.

Although isolated in their neighborhood, the Jews preferred, in the past, to remain inside the walled enclosure which surrounded the palace to benefit from the direct protection of the sultans. The Jewish community held a monopoly on trading in precious metals , which was very productive given that in ancient times buying gold and silver was one of the best ways to save.

The Mellah in the news :

Over the years, the former residents of the Jewish quarter spread throughout the rest of the city and Moroccan merchants took control of the area's markets . Despite this, the most important characteristics that differentiate the Jewish Quarter from Muslim communities can still be appreciated. One of the most important details concerns the construction of the buildings, with exterior wrought-iron lattice balconies, completely different from Moroccan houses , in which the windows face an interior courtyard.

A walk in the Jewish quarter

The best way to discover the Jewish quarter of Fez is to stroll through its main street (rue Mellah) and venture through the narrow streets to reach what were once luxurious fabric markets , which have now become simple clothing stores .

Next to the Royal Palace .
Mausoleum of Moulay Idriss II

The mausoleum (Zaouïa) of Moulay Idriss II is a sanctuary dedicated to the one who was king of Morocco between the years 807 and 828 and founder of the city for the second time in 810.

Moulay Idriss, patron of the city of Fez, is the most revered saint in the country and thousands of Muslims come to the mausoleum to obtain his blessing.

Mausoleum :

Five centuries after the death of Moulay Idriss II, in 1308, a body found in perfect condition, attributed to the patron saint, transformed the region into a sacred place (Zaouia). The mausoleum, located on Place de Marche Verte, began to be built in 1717 and was finished in 1824 , when it has been considered the holiest place in the medina since.

Over the centuries the building has been altered and replaced almost completely. In the 18th century, Moulay Ismail modified it to give it an Alaouite style. The building is part of an architectural ensemble made up of the presumed house of Moulay Idriss, the Al-Araf mosque, the fountain and the house where ablution was performed. The ensemble, known as al-haram (the forbidden), was a place where Muslims could find refuge and asylum . The shrine is open 24 hours a day, always available for hundreds of Muslims from all parts of Morocco to obtain their blessing.

A sacred district :

The streets surrounding the mausoleum make up the famous sacred quarter, delimited by wooden barriers located in the streets, which served to prevent Christians, Jews and donkeys from entering the area .

A glance from the door

As with most places in the city, non-Muslims are prohibited from entering the shrine , but you should go to the gate to see it from the outside and get a small view of the imposing building. From any of the highest points in the city where you can enjoy a panoramic view of Fez, the green pyramidal dome of the Moulay Idriss mausoleum will always stand out.

Near the place en-Nejjarine .
Seffarine Square

The Seffarine Place is a small square in the heart of the medina of Fez el-Bali , between the souk of dyers and that of tanners .

On Place Seffarine, one of the oldest trades in Fez is still preserved today, that of artisan boilermakers . Although the Place Seffarine is best known for the boilermaker trade that is practiced there, you can also discover prestigious monuments such as the Quaraouiyine library and the Seffarine madrasa.

The master boilermakers :

Boilermakers have been engaged in working copper since the dawn of time to produce items such as teapots, trays, pots and other red copper vessels .

The profession in the news :

Although today boilermakers dedicate themselves almost exclusively to repairing old vessels, you will still and always find craftsmen striking metal to give it shape .

North-east of the Fez el-Bali medina, near the Karaouyine mosque .
Medersa Bou Inania

The Medersa Bou Inania, inaugurated in 1350, is one of the Koranic schools of higher education which also served as a residence for students.

Details of the medersa Bou Inania :

Once inside the patio of the Medersa Bou Inania, you will be mesmerized by the walls hand carved on the stucco and wood . The lower part of the walls is covered with small tiles that make up an infinite number of geometric patterns.

Unlike other medersas, the ablution fountain in the inner courtyard is still in use and is used by the hundreds of devotees who continue to come for prayer. From the outside, one of the elements that stands out the most is its tower , which, like the rest of the roof, is covered in shiny green tiles that reflect sunlight.

One of the best maintained :

The Medersa Bou Inania was renovated in the 18th century and in the 20th century. It is therefore currently one of the best preserved in the medina, thus leaving a testimony to the wealth of the ancient city of Fez . Some parts of Bou Inania cannot be visited as it continues to perform its functions as a mosque . Despite this, it is one of the best that can be seen in the city, along with the Medersa Attarine

Madrasa Cherratine

The Medersa Cherratine is one of the many Koranic schools which accommodates its students .

Inaugurated in 1670, the Medersa Cherratine stands out from other medersas in the city by its functional and sparsely decorated style .

Visit of the medersa

At first glance, the Medersa Cherratine looks similar to other madrassas in Fez. Inside there is a large courtyard with a fountain and the three upper floors where the rooms of more than 200 students are located . Unlike the others, the upper part can be visited in order to discover the small rooms in which the students resided. Unfortunately, this madrassa is in rather degraded condition .

This is not one of the best :

Bearing in mind that you are unlikely to visit all the Madrasahs, we believe that the Cherratine Madrasa should not be one of the lucky ones. The Medersa Attarine and the Medersa Bou Inania are in much better condition!

Derb Zaouia, near the Al Quaraouiyine mosque .
Schedule

Every day: from 8:30 a.m. to 12:00 p.m. and from 2:30 p.m. to 6:30 p.m. The Medersa Cherratine will remain closed during prayer times.

Price
General admission: 20 dh (2.20 US $) .
