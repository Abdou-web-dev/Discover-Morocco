Safi...

Safi is a port city located on the Moroccan Atlantic coast. Ksar El Bahr, a 16th-century fortress built by Portuguese settlers, sits on the waterfront. In the old town, food and craft stalls line Rue du Souq. Nearby, the National Ceramics Museum is housed in another fort built by the Portuguese. The centerpieces of the collection are the decorated pottery of the region, created in the workshops of the Colline des Potiers, a hillside district.

What to do in Safi :

1. Visit the La Colline des Potiers

The Potters district occupies the slopes of the hill, where a hundred potters, long ago, settled in an anthill workshops and kilns. At the end of the gallery, the school co-op where you can attend the various stages of manufacture: turning, decorating and cooking. Below, a long shopping arcade presents a complete sampling of production.

2. Stroll at the La Ville de Safi

It should be fortunate to be led by a Moroccan from Safi for a guided tour without asking you one piece. However, many things can be discovered by walking. Get married early so you can stroll with your loving partner as they use to say. You will enjoy the view if ever in La Ville.

3.Bring kids to the Safi Surf Club

The opportunity to spend a little time outside Safi about 15 km and take a breath of fresh air while walking along the beach waiting for the sunset. Ability to surf is what you need to. You can be like the beach boys who surf in the USA like crazy kids.

4.See garden at the Riad du Pêcheur Safi

Safi was listed in the Guinness Book of Records by making the largest Taine of sardine meatballs in the world. Two hundred women participated in the preparation of this huge flat. It would be crazy to hear stories like that in the garden like Riad du Pêcheur Safi, but yes it is true.

5. Try Kite Boarding at Souiria Beach

The beach in Safi is very influenced by the materials carried so much of the continent of trash and waste. by against a lot of greenery on the banks of the river and the dunes which install camps for summer camps. However, there are clean beaches where you can go kiteboarding in Safi.

6. See the Street Art in Safi

The beach is beautiful, the sand is beautiful, the harbor is bustling with fishermen selling their peaches, soles, rays, stones, mullet, etc. You should discover the Moroccan rural life, oil mills are a curiosity of the region, close to Souiria discover the Argan tree, several cooperatives in the area. Street art is common in Safi.

7. Get some colorful pottery in Safi

Pottery, sardines, Portuguese fortifications and hospitality make the Safi a celebrity with clay, rainbow colors, sinuous forms, passion and dexterity of the artisans, that’s roughly what to expect in this craft complex of potters. The objects range from the most basic to gold. Certain private exhibitions are beautiful. The pottery are a thing of beauty in Safi.

8. Have lunch at Le Filet du Pêcheur Numéro 17

The lunch in Morocco can be just as good as anything you have tried in your travel. Try Halal. It is good and healthy. As you all know, don’t be picky with local dishes. Just try. Try local cuisines. It is good for the palate. Moroccan food can be an adventure like in the desert as well.

9. Go see the Kechla

Massive Portuguese-built fortress with ramps, garden courtyards and gunnery platforms. Closed to the public at the time of research.

10. Go see Qasr Al Bahr

The ruins of Safi's once-impressive castle are located next to the crashing waves of the Atlantic. Built to enforce Portuguese authority, house the town governor and protect the port, the tower also once housed prisoners who were kept in the basement before being killed or shipped as slaves. Now in an appalling state of disrepair, the castle is closed to the public while it awaits a long-overdue restoration.

11. Visit Cathédrale Portugaise

Opposite the entrance to the Great Mosque and clearly signed from Rue du Souq, this ruined cathedral dates from 1519 and was built in the Manueline (Portuguese late Gothic) style. Construction was never completed, and most of the structure was demolished after the departure of the Portuguese. All that remains are parts of the choir and a side chapel.

Eljadida..

El-Jadida or al-Jadida - with ancient names al-Brija, Mazagan and al-Mahdouma - is a coastal town in Morocco, 96 km from Casablanca, capital of the province of El Jadida. The fortified city, built by the Portuguese at the beginning of the 16th century and named Mazagan, was taken by the Moroccans in 1769.

What to do in Eljadida :

1. El Jadida Beach

Also known as Deauville Plage, the main town beach is one of El Jadida’s premier summertime attractions. Moroccan families and friends splash about in the waters and relax on the sands. There are sun loungers and parasols available to rent and good facilities close to the beach.

2. Haouzia Beach

Haouzia Beach is a little distance from the town centre and, as such, has fewer summertime crowds. It’s a great place for people looking to relax on a more remote sandy beach. Visitors can also see a partially submerged wreck just off the coast.

3.Sidi Abed Beach

A rather isolated beach, Sidi Abed Beach offers peace and quiet along with scenic views. Pack a picnic and enjoy the tranquil vibe away from the holiday-making crowds.

4.Sidi Bouzid Beach

Sidi Bouzid Beach attracts surf enthusiasts. The golden sands are also ideal for sunbathing and soaking up the sunshine while watching surfers harness the power of the ocean.

5. El Jadida Port

Stroll through the working harbour and watch as vessels come and go, cargo is offloaded, and men repair nets. Colourful boats bob on the waves in the distance.

6. Portuguese Cisterns

Once a warehouse, the Portuguese Cisterns were used to film Orson Welles’ Othello. The gloomy chamber is atmospheric, especially when the sun shines through a hole in the roof and creates reflections on the water and the pillars cast shadows on the walls.

7. Mazagan Fortress

Built in the early 1500s during the Portuguese occupation of the area, the UNESCO-listed Mazagan Fortress is a terrific example of a star-shaped coastal fortress. Old cannons still face out to sea and visitors can wander around the ramparts for great views.

8. Portuguese City Mosque

Although closed to non-Muslims, the Portuguese City Mosque is still an impressive sight from the outside. Its tall minaret looms over the streets below, and the call to prayer is issued from the tall tower five times each day.

9. Chapel of St Sebastian

Also known as the Spanish Chapel, the Chapel of St Sebastian is no longer an active place of worship. Although it has now been converted into a hotel, the renovated façade still looks like it has done for many years.

10. Old synagogue

Located within the fortress remains, the old Jewish synagogue is a reminder of the town’s Jewish heritage. Although it is closed nowadays, it’s still worth seeing from the outside. The Star of David is displayed high above the door.

11. Central Market

One of the town’s main shopping areas for locals, the Central Market is a terrific place to watch locals haggling for an array of goods. While souvenirs are limited, you will still find an assortment of trinkets to take home. Traditional tea sets, lamps, spices, and leather goods also make great gifts.

Oualidia...

Oualidia, or Loualidia, is a town in Morocco located in the province of Sidi Bennour in the region of Casablanca-Settat, on the Atlantic coast, between El Jadida and Safi, 158 km south of Casablanca and 212 km from Marrakech. It constitutes the urban center of the rural municipality of the same name.

What to do in Oualidia:

1. Try this excursion

Go for long walks along the sandy beaches. You will thus enjoy the tranquility of this beautiful lagoon, bordered by cliffs. Precious moments of well-being on the shores of the Atlantic!

2. Try this walk

A sea trip is sure to enchant your stay in Oualidia! On this occasion, you will discover the salt marshes. You will cross very contrasting environments where all kinds of very surprising sea birds live.

3.Try this excursion

Then take a little tour of the oyster farms, and let yourself be tempted by a tasting of these oysters and other seafood for which Oualidia is famous!

4.Try this walk

Notice to amateurs, Oualidia is a paradise for marine sports. Besides, don't miss Surfland! Well supervised by Laurent and his team of instructors, you can learn surfing at any age, but also stand up (paddle) or canoe.

5. Try this excursion

For even more change of scenery, take the coastal road towards Safi: you will cross a landscape of striking contrasts! From market gardens to salt marshes, between desert and ocean, this is an unlikely meeting place between sheep, camels and flamingos.

6. Try and eat oysters

Oualidia is famous for its oyster beds, which produce about 200 tonnes of oysters annually, and this waterfront restaurant attached to an oyster farm is a great place to slurp some bivalves. These come in three grades – the difference is the size. Order them grilled with cheese or chives, or au naturel. Paella and fried fish are also available.

7. Admire Oualidia's Lagoon

Oualidia’s languid lagoon and coastal wetlands is a paradise for birdwatchers, playing host to many bird species, both native and migratory, especially in spring and autumn. Expect to see egrets, plovers, oystercatchers and curlews, and pink flamingos in season. Small wooden boats will take you puttering around its fringes for around Dh50.

Moulay Bousselham...

Moulay Bousselham is a seaside resort in Morocco, located in the region of Rabat-Salé-Kénitra halfway between Rabat and Tangier. The Atlantic coast of Moulay Bousselham is divided into two main beaches: “Hawaii” and “swimming pool”.
Made up of little more than a single street with a smattering of cafés and souvenir shops, the laid-back fishing village of Moulay Bousselham is virtually empty in the cooler months but very popular with Moroccans in summer. Its lagoon and beach are breathtaking, and its sandbar (a potential hazard for swimmers) creates a continual crash of breaking waves.
This is also one of northern Morocco's prime bird-watching locations, with boat trips organized to see thousands of herons, pink flamingos, sheldrakes, and gannets. The whole area is considered a wetland protected by the Ramsar Convention (an international treaty working for the conservation of wetlands) as well as by the village's two patron saints.

What to do in Moulay Bousselham :

1. Go to Merdja Zerga

Moulay Bousselham is at the head of Merdja Zerga (the Blue Lagoon), which gives its name to the surrounding 17,000-acre national park. This region is a major stopover for countless birds migrating from Norway, Sweden, and the United Kingdom to Africa: the birds fly south at the end of summer and stop at Merdja Zerga in September, October, and November before continuing on to West Africa and even South Africa. They stop off again on their way back to Europe in spring, so spring and fall are the times for bird-watching. The pink flamingos on their way to and from Mauritania are particularly spectacular.

2. Visit Moulay Bousselham's Tomb

At the foot of the village, near the sea, you'll find the tomb of Moulay Bousselham. Like Sidi Abdel Jalil's somewhat smaller one, it is a white building capped with a dome.

Larache...

The history of the province begins with the famous city of Lixus, an ancient city founded by the Phoenicians around -1100 whose ruins are located a few kilometers north of the small town of Larache in Morocco, on the right bank of the wadi Loukkos.
With the Roman withdrawal from North Africa and the victory of Islam in North Africa, Lixus slowly declined in favor of the new town of Larache, erected on the other side of the Loukkos. However, archaeological excavations have revealed the existence of a first Muslim nucleus near the historic site of Lixus (mosque).

What to do in Larache :

1. Lixus Ruins

Set on a hill with spectacular views over the Loukos Estuary, the Carthaginian and Roman ruins of Lixus are evocative reminders that settlements on this coast are among the oldest in the country. And while it lacks the grandeur of Volubilis – much of the ruins have been eroded by time and the elements – you’ll be able to wander without the crowds. And now there’s a contemporary visitor centre with a fascinating display charting what’s known of the site’s history.

2. Medina

Entered through the Bab Al Khemis, an imposing Hispano-Moorish structure, Larache's blue-and-white medina has changed little over the years. Mostly residential, it's arranged around the Zoco de la Alcaiceria, a colonnaded marketplace, where fresh produce and household goods are sold from open-fronted stores, wooden carts or straight off the cobbles. To the north is a maze of narrow lanes leading to the ruined Saadian Fortress. South, up the hill, is the handsome Music Conservatory and a scenic lookout.

3.Place de la Libération

Built by the Spanish, who called it Plaza de España (some locals still do), this grand oval-shaped plaza is the town's focal point. Decorated with palm trees and a fountain, it's encircled by handsome Hispano-Moorish buildings where terrace cafes are a favourite haunt for locals. On the eastern side, the magnificent, glaze-tiled Bab Al Khemis is the centrepiece of an arched walkway and leads to the medina’s marketplace.

4.Ras R'mel Beach

Larache has a small rubbish-strewn strip of sand below the town, but the best beach is across the Loukos Estuary, an 11km drive from the town centre. In summer, small boats ferry passengers across the estuary, from where the beach is a short walk across the dunes. You'll need to bargain with the boatman to get the best price (around Dh10). At other times a petit taxi from the town centre will cost around Dh25.

5. Lookout

With stunning views over the port and estuary, this lookout is a popular meeting spot. On the way you'll pass the crumbling 17th-century fortification Kasbah de la Cigogne, built by the Spaniards under Philip III, and the old town's landmark mosque (closed to non-Muslims).

6. Jean Genet’s Grave

French literature buffs mays want to head to the old Spanish cemetery, the final resting place of Jean Genet (1910–86). If the gate is locked, ring the bell for the caretaker. A small tip is expected for showing you to the grave.
